1. Topic Selection

Project Title: Single Axis Solar Tracker with Automated Speed Breaker

In the realm of sustainable engineering, two pressing issues often dominate discussions: the inefficiency of static renewable energy sources and the need for smarter, safer urban infrastructure. Our project was conceived at this intersection.

We chose to integrate a Solar Tracking System‚Äîtraditionally used in large solar farms‚Äîwith a novel application: a Speed-Sensing Traffic Breaker. This "hybrid" approach ensures that the power required for the smart traffic system is generated entirely on-site, creating a self-sustaining unit ideal for smart cities and remote highways alike.

2. Aim & Objectives

The primary aim was to design a robust, low-cost embedded system that enhances energy harvest efficiency while contributing to public safety.

Energy Efficiency

To increase the photovoltaic (PV) panel's output by 25-30% compared to static panels by maintaining a perpendicular angle to the sun's rays throughout the day.

Traffic Safety

To automate speed breakers so they only deploy for speeding vehicles, reducing wear on compliant vehicles and improving traffic flow.

3. Components & Logic

The system architecture relies on a closed-loop feedback control system involving sensors, a controller, and actuators.

‚òÄÔ∏è
A. Light Detection (LDRs)

We utilized two Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs) placed on the East and West edges of the panel. They function as variable resistors in a potential divider circuit. When light intensity varies, the voltage drop across them changes, providing an analog signal (0-5V) to the Arduino's comparator logic.

‚öôÔ∏è
B. Mechanical Actuation (Servo Motors)

SG90 Micro Servos were chosen for their precise angular control. The primary servo receives PWM signals to tilt the solar panel, ensuring the LDRs receive equal light intensity (equilibrium state). A secondary servo actuates the speed breaker mechanism.

üß†
C. Control Unit (Arduino Nano)

The ATmega328P based Nano acts as the brain. It constantly samples the LDR values. If |LDR1 - LDR2| > Threshold, it adjusts the servo position. Simultaneously, it monitors the Ultrasonic sensor to calculate vehicle velocity ($v = d/t$).

4. Problems & Solutions

During the prototyping phase, we encountered several engineering challenges that required iterative problem-solving.

Challenge 1: Parasitic Oscillation (Jitter)

Issue: In initial tests, the solar panel would vibrate continuously when light levels were similar on both sides, or due to passing clouds. This "hunting" behavior wasted power and heated up the servo.

Engineering Solution: We implemented a software Hysteresis Loop (Tolerance Band). The code logic was updated to only trigger movement if the difference between LDR values exceeded a specific threshold (e.g., 50 units). This created a "dead zone" where the system remains stable.

Challenge 2: Power Instability

Issue: When the servo motors engaged, they drew high current spikes, causing the voltage to drop and the Arduino to reset unexpectedly (Brownout).

Engineering Solution: We designed a Dual-Rail Power Supply. The motors were powered by a dedicated 9V high-current source via the L293D driver, while the Arduino was isolated on a regulated 5V rail. We also added decoupling capacitors to filter noise.

5. Innovation

Smart Integration: The "Eco-Safe" Concept

While solar trackers and speed breakers exist individually, our innovation lies in their symbiotic integration.

  • ‚ú¶ Zero-Grid Dependency: By powering the speed breaker's servo motor directly from the tracked solar panel's battery storage, we eliminated the need for external grid connections. This makes the system deployable in remote highway locations.
  • ‚ú¶ Dynamic Traffic Flow: Traditional speed breakers are passive obstacles. Our active system uses Ultrasonic logic to differentiate between safe and speeding drivers, "rewarding" compliance with a smooth road surface and "punishing" violations with a physical barrier.
  • ‚ú¶ Low-Cost Scalability: By utilizing off-the-shelf components like LDRs instead of complex pyranometers, and simple Ultrasonic sensors instead of radar guns, we achieved a prototype cost under ‚Çπ2000, making mass adoption financially viable.

Circuit Explanation

Detailed Connection Logic

Connection Steps

  1. Input Power: A 9V DC source powers the entire control circuit.
  2. Signal Processing: This source connects to an LM358N IC, which acts as a dual operational amplifier.
  3. Motor Drive: The Op-Amp output pin acts as the input trigger for the motor driver.
  4. Actuation: The motor driver connects directly to the DC gear motor for panel rotation.
  5. Sensors: Two LDRs are connected in series with two fixed resistors. Each LDR is paired with one resistor.
  6. Divider Logic: This arrangement forms a voltage divider circuit. The voltage at the middle point varies with light intensity.
  7. Indicators:
    • Blue LED (Beside Solar): Indicates solar panel is active.
    • Two Bottom LEDs: Indicate orientation (Left/Right movement).
  8. Charging Control: A MOSFET acts as a switch/channel between two capacitors, allowing them to charge only when sufficient light falls on the solar panel.
